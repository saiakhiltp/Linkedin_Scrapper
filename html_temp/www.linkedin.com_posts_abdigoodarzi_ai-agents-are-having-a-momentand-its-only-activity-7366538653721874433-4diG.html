How AI agents are revolutionizing workflows and tasks

This title was summarized by AI from the post below.
Abdi Goodarzi
2mo Edited
  • Report this post

AI agents are having a moment—and it’s only getting bigger.    As Nitin Mittal and I share in Forbes (https://deloi.tt/47evAU7), we’ve moved from foundational LLMs that just “chat” with data (think: generating content or code) to AI agents that can actually reason, design workflows and take action. These aren’t just smart chatbots—they’re autonomous digital workers that can process tons of data, understand context and even collaborate with each other to get complex stuff done.    Picture this: Instead of the old-school, linear workflow (a team member collects data, another analyzes, and then eventually a leader makes a decision), AI agents can streamline the whole process. These digital workers can handle transactions, analyze trends, make recommendations, and even execute tasks—freeing up humans to focus on creative problem-solving and big-picture strategy.    From Finance to Sales & Marketing to Procurement, we’re seeing that agents can have a serious impact on automating key workflows and tackling complex tasks. And that’s just the beginning - agents can also play a big role in advancing customer experience, recruitment and HR.    The bottom line: AI agents are here to help us work smarter, not harder. But trust is key—transparency, fairness, and accountability should be built in from day one. Check out our article to see how you can get started.

    AI isn’t replacing people. It’s replacing bad processes. If you want ROI, don’t start with a chatbot. Start with the 10 tiny frustrations YOUR team hits every hour. We can call it the friction tax. Common ones: copy-pasting account IDs, hunting 5 webpages or documents for one answer, writing the same note 300 times, chasing approvals, formatting refunds. How I make AI useful without hype: 1. Baseline the pain: If you don't know the pain points of your team, how will you help them. Determine time to find an answer, clicks per ticket, edits to a macro. 2. Ship one narrow assist: Don’t try to roll out a giant AI overhaul all at once. Start with one tiny, clearly defined task that AI can reliably help with, and “ship” (launch) that into production first. Auto-surface the customer record when they chat or call, summarize the last 5 interactions, prefill forms, suggest a reply for your top 5 intents. 3. Keep a human in the loop: Very important, especially in the beginning. Letting AI run free (just like letting a brand new employee run free without any oversight) will kill your company. An agent accept or edit. Track acceptance rate and edit distance. 4. Set hard guardrails: No refunds, no policy changes, ALWAYS log prompts and outputs. 5. Review weekly, then widen: if the metric moves, expand. If not, fix the workflow, not the model. Targets: Save 30-60 seconds per ticket within 2 weeks. Agent acceptance rate or AI activity above 60 percent. Edit distance below 40 percent (hopefully much less). Zero critical errors. AI doesn’t make people obsolete (right now). It removes the junk work so good people can do great work. #AIinCX #Operations #CustomerSupport #Leadership #ProcessDesign

    Pros and Cons of implementing AI in Businesses at this time: ✅ Pros of AI for Businesses 1. Efficiency & Automation Automates repetitive, manual tasks (e.g., data entry, reporting, scheduling). Frees employees to focus on higher-value work. Reduces error rates in routine processes. 2. Cost Savings Cuts labor costs in areas where automation is effective. Optimizes resource usage (energy, inventory, logistics). Predictive maintenance lowers downtime and repair costs in manufacturing. 3. Data-Driven Insights AI can analyze massive amounts of data quickly. Identifies trends, customer behavior patterns, and predictive forecasts. Supports better strategic decision-making. 4. Customer Experience 24/7 chatbots and virtual assistants improve response times. Personalized product recommendations drive sales and loyalty. Enhanced customer segmentation and targeting for marketing campaigns. 5. Competitive Advantage Early adopters can outpace competitors with faster innovation. AI enables new business models (e.g., subscription-based predictive services). Helps small and mid-sized companies “punch above their weight.” ⚠️ Cons of AI for Businesses 1. Upfront Costs & Complexity High initial investment in software, infrastructure, and integration. Skilled talent (data scientists, AI engineers) can be expensive and scarce. Small businesses may struggle with budget and expertise. 2. Data Dependency & Quality Issues AI systems require large amounts of clean, accurate data. Poor or biased data can lead to flawed decisions. Privacy and security concerns with sensitive information. 3. Workforce Impact Potential job displacement for repetitive and routine roles. Employees may resist adoption due to fear of being replaced. Requires ongoing reskilling and training. 4. Ethical & Legal Risks Bias in AI models can result in unfair outcomes (hiring, lending, etc.). Lack of transparency in decision-making (“black box” problem). Growing regulatory scrutiny (e.g., GDPR, AI Act). 5. Reliability & Trust AI systems can make mistakes or fail in unexpected scenarios. Over-reliance on AI without human oversight can harm decision quality. Maintaining trust with customers requires transparency and accountability. 👉 In short: AI accelerates growth, reduces costs, and improves insights—but it comes with challenges around cost, ethics, workforce disruption, and reliability. The key is balanced adoption: combining AI automation with human judgment and oversight.

    AI agents are spreading fast and with that comes the big question... what’s left for humans? On one side you’ve got people worried about job security. On the other, sky-high expectations about productivity. The truth is somewhere in the middle of these two extremes. Yes, agents can handle a lot. However humans remain critical, especially in enterprise marketing where they are essential too; > Overseeing accuracy and brand compliance > Applying judgment to campaign nuances > Handling edge cases AI can’t anticipate > Deciding when an agent isn’t the right tool at all Here's a client example; a global marketing team redesigned its campaign-planning workflow with agents. The AI agent could draft campaign structures and organise budget allocations with impressive accuracy. Though marketers still had to: > Review and sign off plans (the budget was too important to leave unchecked) > Adjust recommendations based on market context and brand priorities > Step in on edge cases where data signals conflicted Crucially, simple interfaces were built that let humans and agents collaborate e.g. highlights, bounding boxes, click-to-scroll functionality that made validation easy. This resulted in faster reviews, fewer mistakes, and adoption rates above 90%. My strongly held view is that success with AI agents isn’t about replacement. It’s about redesigning workflows so people and agents can work together without friction. Ignore that, and you risk quiet failures and rejected tools, but if you get it right, then you unlock speed, accuracy, and trust.

    Most agencies are testing “AI agents” as a way to scale human work. But the reality is mixed. Instead of one agent doing everything, they are building small task-specific agents: one for research, one for outreach, one for reporting. Each handles narrow tasks, then hands off to the next. This looks powerful on paper—24/7 execution, faster throughput, lower marginal cost. But the bottleneck often remains: coordination. Agencies still need a layer of human oversight to review outputs, avoid duplication, and connect the dots between agents. The agencies seeing real gains are the ones designing workflows where AI agents don’t just replace humans but extend them. - Lead research agents feeding enriched data into CRM. - Outreach agents generating drafts aligned with brand voice. - Analytics agents flagging anomalies in campaign performance before humans even notice. AI agents are not a plug-and-play workforce. They are building blocks. The advantage comes from how you connect them. Are we moving toward agencies with fewer humans—or agencies where humans orchestrate dozens of specialized AI workers? At TriggerBI, we help companies design and automate end-to-end processes with the right balance of human input and AI execution.
